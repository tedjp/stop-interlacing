What is interlacing?

Interlacing is a way of transmitting video half a frame at a time. It's a relic from the days of analog TV and low-compression codecs, where a reasonably good picture could be transmitted using about half the bandwidth by transmitting alternating lines of each frame.

The problem with interlacing

Modern displays refresh the entire picture in one go. When the source video is interlaced it only contains half a new picture. The display either retains the existing half of the picture, resulting in a "comb" effect on the edges of objects, or it employs imperfect deinterlacing algorithms to try to reconstruct a full frame. Having only half the picture each frame means the deinterlacer is always trying to reconstruct detail for which it has no information, and therefore the picture looks worse than a full-frame (progressive) picture.

The effect is especially egregious for sports content where the object of focus tends to be a small, fast-moving object. As it moves across the screen, a ball is only half-displayed at a time. Moving fast enough, the deinterlacer won't identify the two half-balls as the same moving object (because it travels so far between frames) and the ball will be half-invisible.

The solution: progressive video

Progressive, full-frame video doesn't have the problem of trying to reconstruct missing information. Video providers should commit to an end-to-end progressive video pipeline. For example 2160p, 1080p or even 720p. 1080i is insufficient, and especially poor for sports content.
